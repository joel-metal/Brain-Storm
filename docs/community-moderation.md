# Community Moderation Guide

## Moderation roles and permissions

A clear moderation model ensures consistent enforcement and protects the community.

- **Administrators**: full platform control, manage moderator privileges, and act on escalations. They can remove content, ban accounts, and update moderation policy.
- **Moderators**: review flagged content, resolve disputes, and remove or escalate posts that violate guidelines. They should have access to moderation tools and audit records.
- **Support staff**: handle appeals, help users understand policy, and coordinate with moderators for edge cases.
- **Community volunteers**: may provide trusted guidance or flag content, but should not have unilateral removal powers unless explicitly authorized.

Permissions should be scoped so that only appropriate roles can remove content, ban users, or change moderation settings. Audit logging is required for every moderation action.

## Content flagging and removal process

The removal workflow should be clear and transparent:

1. **Flagging**: users can flag posts, comments, or messages for review. Flags should include categories such as spam, harassment, copyright, or off-topic.
2. **Triage**: the moderation queue assigns flags to moderators based on severity and workload.
3. **Review**: moderators inspect the content, the user history, and relevant context.
4. **Action**:
   - **Dismiss** if the content complies with policy.
   - **Edit** if the content can be corrected without removal.
   - **Remove** if the content violates rules.
   - **Suspend or ban** repeat offenders or serious violations.
5. **Notify**: inform the user when content is removed or action is taken, including the reason and next steps.
6. **Escalate**: for complex cases, escalate to administrators or legal/compliance as needed.

## Community guidelines

The community should follow a shared set of expectations:

- Be respectful and constructive.
- Stay on-topic and relevant to the course or discussion.
- Avoid hate speech, harassment, discrimination, or personal attacks.
- Do not post spam, phishing links, or unsolicited promotion.
- Respect copyright and intellectual property.
- Keep private or sensitive information out of public discussions.
- Report harmful or abusive behavior through flags instead of responding directly.

Publish these guidelines prominently in forum terms of use and community help pages.

## Spam detection strategies

Automated spam detection complements human moderation.

- **Rate-based heuristics**: flag accounts that post too frequently or create many new threads in a short period.
- **Content patterns**: detect repeated text, suspicious URLs, or common spam keywords.
- **Reputation signals**: apply stricter checks to new accounts and allow more trusted users more leeway.
- **Machine learning / scoring**: use spam-detection services or custom classifiers for suspicious content.
- **Blacklist matching**: block known malicious domains, disposable email signatures, and abusive IP ranges.
- **Captcha / challenge flows**: require additional verification for suspicious submissions.

Combine automated signals with manual review to reduce false positives and keep the community experience positive.

## Appeal process

Users should be able to appeal moderation actions in a structured way:

1. **Notice**: provide a clear explanation when content is removed or an account is restricted.
2. **Appeal submission**: offer a simple way for users to request review, such as a support ticket or appeal form.
3. **Review window**: moderators or support staff should evaluate appeals within a defined timeframe (for example, 48 hours).
4. **Outcome**:
   - **Uphold** the original decision and explain why.
   - **Reverse** the action if the content was incorrectly removed.
   - **Adjust** the enforcement if the policy needs refinement.
5. **Follow-up**: communicate the final decision clearly and update community guidance if systemic issues are discovered.

Appeals should be logged and reviewed periodically to improve moderation quality.
